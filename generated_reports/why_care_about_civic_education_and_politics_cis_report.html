Why Care About Civic Education and Politics? The Price of Apathy is Governance by Thieves

LEDE: The Matatu Driver Who Paid for Your Silence

You board a matatu at 6:30 a.m., bleary-eyed, clutching your phone like a life preserver. The driver swerves through potholes the size of bathtubs, cursing under his breath. You don’t ask why the road is a death trap. You don’t ask who pocketed the money meant for repairs. You just grip your seat, pray, and scroll. By the time you reach the office, you’ve already paid twice: once in fare, once in dignity. That’s the cost of apathy. The matatu driver didn’t steal your money. The system did. And you let it.

NUT GRAF: Because the Constitution Isn’t a Suggestion:It’s Your Job Description

Kenya’s 2010 Constitution didn’t just redraw boundaries. It redrew power. Article 1 doesn’t say, “All sovereign power belongs to the people of Kenya, but only if they feel like using it.” It says, “All sovereign power belongs to the people of Kenya and shall be exercised only in accordance with this Constitution.” That’s not a loophole. That’s a command. Civic education isn’t a luxury. It’s the toolkit for the job you didn’t know you signed up for: citizen-in-chief. This article isn’t here to guilt you. It’s here to remind you that every time you ignore politics, you’re not just opting out. You’re handing the keys to the vault to someone who will rob you blind:and laugh while doing it.

Here’s the roadmap:

  1. We’ll start with the cold, hard numbers: how apathy turns into billions lost.
  2. Then, we’ll dissect the Constitution like a surgeon:because it’s not just a document, it’s your weapon.
  3. We’ll expose the thieves: not just the men in suits, but the culture that lets them thrive.
  4. Finally, we’ll give you the playbook. Not vague platitudes, but concrete steps to turn your outrage into power.

PART 1: THE BILLION-SHILLING QUESTION:WHERE DID YOUR MONEY GO?

1.1 The Math of Theft: How Apathy Fuels the Fire

In 2022, Kenya lost KSh 1.1 trillion to corruption. That’s not a typo. That’s 11 followed by eleven zeros. To put it in perspective: that’s enough to build 11,000 km of tarmacked roads:nearly three times the distance from Nairobi to Cape Town. Or, if you prefer, it’s enough to pay the salaries of 220,000 teachers for a decade. [DATA: Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission (EACC) Annual Report, 2022]

But here’s the kicker: corruption isn’t just about the money stolen. It’s about the money never spent. Every shilling siphoned off is a shilling not spent on hospitals, schools, or clean water. It’s a shilling that could have paid for your child’s textbooks, your mother’s medication, or the bridge that would have saved your uncle’s life when his car plunged into a river because the county government “forgot” to fix the guardrail. Apathy isn’t passive. It’s an accomplice.

1.2 The Silent Tax: How Ignorance Costs You More Than Bribes

You think you’re not paying for corruption? Think again. The average Kenyan pays 16 bribes a year, totaling KSh 12,000 annually. That’s not pocket change. That’s a school fee. A month’s rent. A bag of maize flour every month for a year. [DATA: Transparency International Kenya, 2023]

But the real silent tax isn’t the bribes. It’s the opportunity cost. When a county government awards a tender to a ghost company, that’s not just a line item in a budget. That’s a clinic that won’t be built. A borehole that won’t be drilled. A road that won’t be graded. And when those things don’t happen, you pay. You pay in time lost waiting for an ambulance that never comes. You pay in crops rotting because the road to market is impassable. You pay in your child’s future, because the school they attend has no books, no desks, and a teacher who hasn’t been paid in six months.

This isn’t abstract. This is your life. And the people stealing from you? They’re counting on you to stay quiet.

PART 2: THE CONSTITUTION ISN’T A SUGGESTION:IT’S YOUR WEAPON

2.1 Article 1: The Clause That Makes You the Boss

Let’s talk about Article 1. Not the watered-down version you hear in political speeches. The real one. The one that says, “All sovereign power belongs to the people of Kenya and shall be exercised only in accordance with this Constitution.” That’s not a feel-good statement. That’s a legal transfer of power. It means that every politician, every civil servant, every chief, every policeman:they work for you. Not the other way around.

But here’s the catch: power isn’t like a matatu. You don’t just hop in and expect the driver to take you where you want to go. You have to steer. And to steer, you need to know the rules of the road. That’s where civic education comes in. It’s not about memorizing clauses. It’s about understanding that the Constitution isn’t just a document. It’s a contract. And like any contract, if you don’t read the fine print, you’re going to get screwed.

2.2 Public Participation: The Loophole Thieves Don’t Want You to Know About

Buried in the Constitution is a little-known clause called public participation. It’s in Article 10 (national values) and Article 174 (objects of devolution). It’s the reason county governments are required to hold public forums before passing budgets. It’s the reason you have the right to demand that your ward administrator explains why your neighborhood’s streetlights haven’t worked in two years.

But here’s the problem: most Kenyans don’t know this. And the ones who do? They don’t use it. In 2021, only 3% of Kenyans participated in public budget hearings. Three percent. That means 97% of us handed over our power to a room full of people who don’t live in our neighborhoods, don’t send their kids to our schools, and don’t drink our water. [DATA: Institute of Economic Affairs (IEA) Kenya, 2021]

Public participation isn’t a courtesy. It’s a constitutional right. And when you don’t show up, you’re not just missing a meeting. You’re signing a blank check.

2.3 The Power of the Purse: Why Your County Budget is Your Business

Let’s say your county government allocates KSh 500 million for road construction. On paper, that’s great. But what if KSh 200 million of that goes to a company owned by the governor’s cousin? What if the roads are built with substandard materials, so they crumble after the first rain? What if the tender was awarded without competitive bidding, in violation of Article 227 of the Constitution?

If you don’t know the budget exists, you can’t ask these questions. If you don’t ask these questions, the theft continues. And if the theft continues, you keep paying the silent tax. The one that shows up in your life as a pothole, a power outage, or a hospital with no medicine.

Here’s the good news: budgets are public documents. You have the right to see them. You have the right to question them. You have the right to demand answers. But you can’t exercise those rights if you don’t know they exist.

PART 3: THE THIEVES IN THE ROOM:AND THE CULTURE THAT PROTECTS THEM

3.1 The Usual Suspects: Politicians, Bureaucrats, and the Men Who Love Them

Let’s be clear: the primary thieves are the ones in power. The governors who award tenders to their cronies. The MPs who vote for budgets they know are padded. The civil servants who demand bribes to do the jobs they’re already paid to do. These are the people who treat public office like a personal ATM.

But they don’t operate in a vacuum. They operate in a culture that enables them. A culture where we shrug and say, “Hii ni Kenya.” A culture where we treat corruption like the weather:something we complain about but can’t change. A culture where we’d rather share a meme about a scandal than show up to a public hearing about it.

Here’s the truth: the thieves aren’t just the ones in suits. They’re the ones who look the other way. They’re the ones who say, “Politics is dirty, so I won’t get involved.” They’re the ones who think their vote doesn’t matter, so they don’t register. They’re the ones who let the matatu driver swerve through potholes without asking why.

3.2 The Myth of the “Good Guy”: Why Your Favorite Politician is Probably Part of the Problem

We love our “good guys.” The MP who built a school. The governor who fixed a road. The senator who “speaks truth to power.” But here’s the thing: no politician is an island. Even the “good guys” operate in a system that rewards corruption. And if they’re not actively fighting to change that system, they’re part of the problem.

Take the MP who built a school. Great, right? But what if that school was built with money that should have gone to five other schools? What if the contractor was his brother-in-law, who overcharged by 30%? What if the school has no teachers because the county government “forgot” to budget for salaries? Suddenly, the “good guy” doesn’t look so good.

The point isn’t that all politicians are evil. The point is that no politician is perfect. And if you’re not holding them accountable, you’re not just letting them off the hook. You’re giving them permission to steal.

3.3 The Real Enemy: The Belief That You Can’t Make a Difference

The biggest lie in Kenyan politics isn’t that corruption is inevitable. It’s that you can’t do anything about it. That’s the lie the thieves want you to believe. Because if you believe it, you won’t show up. You won’t ask questions. You won’t demand answers. And if you don’t do those things, they can keep stealing.

But here’s the truth: you have more power than you think. You have the power to vote. The power to attend public hearings. The power to demand transparency. The power to hold leaders accountable. The power to say, “No more.”

The question is: will you use it?

PART 4: THE PLAYBOOK:HOW TO TURN OUTRAGE INTO POWER

4.1 Step 1: Know Your Rights (And How to Use Them)

You can’t fight what you don’t understand. So start with the basics. Read Article 1 of the Constitution. Memorize it. Tattoo it on your forearm if you have to. It’s your job description as a citizen. Then, move on to Article 35 (access to information) and Article 201 (public finance). These aren’t just legal clauses. They’re your tools.

Next, find out how your county government works. Who’s your ward administrator? When are the next public budget hearings? What’s the process for filing a complaint about a pothole or a bribe? Information is power. And in Kenya, power is the only thing thieves respect.

4.2 Step 2: Show Up (Or Get Played)

Public participation isn’t a spectator sport. You can’t tweet about corruption from your couch and expect things to change. You have to show up. Attend the budget hearings. Ask questions. Demand answers. If the county government “forgets” to advertise the hearings, demand they reschedule. If they try to rush through the agenda, insist on more time. If they ignore you, escalate. Write to the county assembly. Write to the media. Write to the Ethics and Anti-Corruption Commission (EACC).

Here’s the thing: thieves hate attention. They hate accountability. They hate the idea that someone might actually be watching. So be that someone. Show up. Speak up. Don’t let them get away with it.

4.3 Step 3: Vote Like Your Life Depends on It (Because It Does)

Voting isn’t just about marking a ballot. It’s about choosing who gets to make decisions about your life. And if you don’t vote, you’re letting someone else make those decisions for you. Someone who doesn’t know you. Someone who doesn’t care about you. Someone who might be a thief.

But voting isn’t enough. You have to vote smart. That means researching the candidates. Not just their promises, but their track records. Have they ever been accused of corruption? Have they ever been involved in a scandal? Do they have a history of showing up to work, or are they perennial absentees? If you don’t know the answers to these questions, you’re not ready to vote.

And don’t just vote for the president. Vote for your MP. Vote for your MCA. Vote for your governor. These are the people who control your day-to-day life. The ones who decide whether your road gets fixed, your hospital gets medicine, your school gets teachers. Don’t hand them the keys to your life without doing your homework.

4.4 Step 4: Hold Them Accountable (Or Watch Them Steal)

Voting is just the first step. The real work starts after the election. That’s when you hold your leaders accountable. That means tracking their promises. If they said they’d build a road, ask for updates. If they said they’d hire more teachers, demand a timeline. If they said they’d fight corruption, ask for receipts.

It also means reporting corruption when you see it. If a civil servant asks for a bribe, report them to the EACC. If a contractor is cutting corners, report them to the county government. If a politician is embezzling funds, report them to the media. Don’t assume someone else will do it. Be that someone.

And if your leaders ignore you? Make noise. Organize protests. Write op-eds. Start a petition. Use social media. Do whatever it takes to get their attention. Because the only thing thieves hate more than accountability is bad publicity.

4.5 Step 5: Educate Others (Because Apathy is Contagious)

You can’t fight corruption alone. You need an army. So start educating the people around you. Your family. Your friends. Your neighbors. Your colleagues. Teach them about their rights. Show them how to access information. Encourage them to show up to public hearings. Help them register to vote.

And don’t just preach to the choir. Talk to the people who think politics doesn’t affect them. The ones who say, “I don’t care about politics.” The ones who think their vote doesn’t matter. Show them how corruption affects their lives. Show them how apathy enables theft. Show them that they have the power to make a difference.

Because the truth is, apathy is contagious. But so is action. And if enough of us start acting, we can change the culture. We can make corruption unacceptable. We can make accountability the norm. We can take back our country.

WITTY OUTTAKE: The Day the Matatu Driver Learned to Ask Why

Remember that matatu driver from the beginning? The one swerving through potholes while you scrolled on your phone? Let’s imagine a different ending.

This time, you don’t just grip your seat and pray. This time, you ask: “Bwana, why are there so many potholes?” The driver sighs. “Hii ni Kenya,” he says. But you don’t accept that. You pull out your phone and open the county budget. You show him the line item for road repairs. You show him the tender awarded to the governor’s cousin. You show him the public hearing notice he missed because no one advertised it.

The driver’s eyes widen. “So they stole the money?” he asks. You nod. “And no one did anything?” You shake your head. “Not yet.”

The next day, the driver shows up to the county assembly. He’s not alone. He’s brought his matatu crew. His passengers. His neighbors. They demand answers. They demand accountability. And for the first time, the politicians listen.

That’s the power of civic education. It turns victims into bosses. It turns apathy into action. It turns potholes into progress. And it starts with one simple question: Why?

So the next time you’re in a matatu, don’t just scroll. Ask why. And don’t stop until you get an answer. Because the price of apathy isn’t just a bumpy ride. It’s a country run by thieves. And that’s a price none of us can afford to pay.
