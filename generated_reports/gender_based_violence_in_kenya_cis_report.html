Gender Based Violence in Kenya: When the Constitution Whispers and Society Roars

1. The Lede: A Morning Like Any Other

It is 5:30 a.m. in Kibera. The matatu touts are already shouting, the smell of mandazi frying mixes with the stench of open sewers, and 16-year-old Amina is sprinting home, her school bag bouncing against her back. She is late. Again. Not because she overslept, but because she had to wait until her uncle left the house:her uncle who, since her father died, has taken to visiting her room at night. She tells no one. Not her mother, who is too busy selling vegetables to notice the bruises on her arms. Not her teacher, who marks her absent when she skips school to avoid him. Amina knows the Constitution says she has the right to freedom and security of the person [CEKA_SRC: Article 29]. She knows the law says she is equal before the law [CEKA_SRC: Article 27]. But in the cold light of dawn, those words feel like whispers in a storm. The storm is her reality: a society that still treats women and girls as property, not people.

2. The Nut Graf: Why This Matters Now

Kenya’s Constitution is one of the most progressive in Africa. It guarantees equality, dignity, and freedom from violence. Yet, every day, women and girls like Amina live in a country where the law is a shield with holes:big enough for fists, insults, and impunity to slip through. This is not just a story about violence. It is a story about the gap between the promise of the Constitution and the reality on the ground. It is about why, despite the ink on paper, Gender Based Violence (GBV) remains a national emergency. And it is about what happens when the state fails to protect its citizens, leaving them to fight battles the Constitution was meant to win for them.

In this article, we will:

  • Examine the constitutional protections against GBV and why they are not enough.
  • Expose the societal norms that fuel violence and how they collide with the law.
  • Break down the numbers to show the scale of the crisis.
  • Ask the hard questions: Where is the state? Why are perpetrators walking free? And what can the common mwananchi do about it?

This is not just another report. This is a call to action for the BOSS:the Kenyan citizen who refuses to accept that violence is the price of being a woman or girl in this country.

3. The Constitutional Promise: A Shield with Holes

Article 27: The Equality Clause That Society Ignores

The Constitution is clear: "Equality includes the full and equal enjoyment of all rights and fundamental freedoms" [CEKA_SRC: Article 27(1)]. It goes further: "Women and men have the right to equal treatment, including the right to equal opportunities in political, economic, cultural, and social spheres" [CEKA_SRC: Article 27(3)]. On paper, this is revolutionary. In practice, it is a mirage for millions of Kenyan women.

Take the workplace. The law says no one should be discriminated against based on gender. Yet, women earn 30% less than men for the same work [DATA: Kenya National Bureau of Statistics, 2022]. In politics, women hold just 23% of parliamentary seats, despite the Constitution’s two-thirds gender rule [DATA: Inter-Parliamentary Union, 2023]. And in the home? The law says women have the right to own property, but cultural practices in many communities still bar them from inheriting land. If the Constitution is a shield, it is one that society refuses to hold up.

Article 29: The Right to Freedom from Violence

Article 29 of the Constitution is unambiguous: "Every person has the right to freedom and security of the person, which includes the right not to be subjected to any form of violence from either public or private sources" [CEKA_SRC: Article 29(c)]. This is not just a right; it is a command. The state is obligated to protect its citizens from violence, whether it comes from a stranger on the street or a family member at home. Yet, in Kenya, violence against women and girls is not just common:it is normalized.

Consider this: 45% of Kenyan women aged 15-49 have experienced physical violence, and 14% have experienced sexual violence [DATA: Kenya Demographic and Health Survey, 2022]. These are not just numbers. They are stories of women who trusted the system to protect them, only to be failed. The police station that turns them away. The court that delays their case for years. The chief who tells them to "go home and solve it with your husband." The Constitution says they have the right to be free from violence. Society says, "Shika hiyo."

4. The Societal Reality: When Culture Eats the Constitution for Breakfast

The Myth of "Private Matters"

In Kenya, the home is often the most dangerous place for a woman. Yet, when violence happens behind closed doors, society shrugs and calls it a "private matter." This is the first lie that fuels GBV. The Constitution does not recognize "private matters." It recognizes rights. And when a man beats his wife, it is not a private matter:it is a crime. But try telling that to the police officer who laughs when a woman reports her husband. Or the village elder who orders her to "endure for the sake of the children."

The normalization of domestic violence is so deep that many women do not even recognize it as violence. A 2021 study by the Federation of Women Lawyers (FIDA-Kenya) found that 38% of women believe a husband is justified in beating his wife for reasons like burning food or refusing sex [DATA: FIDA-Kenya, 2021]. This is not just a failure of the law. It is a failure of society to value women as human beings.

The Blame Game: Victim Shaming as a National Pastime

When a woman is raped, the first question is not "Who did this?" but "What was she wearing?" When a girl is assaulted, the first instinct is to ask, "Why was she out so late?" This is the second lie that fuels GBV: the idea that women are responsible for the violence committed against them. The Constitution says no one should be subjected to violence [CEKA_SRC: Article 29]. It does not say, "Unless you were wearing a short skirt."

Victim blaming is not just a cultural problem. It is a systemic one. In 2020, a survey by the National Crime Research Centre found that 62% of Kenyans believe women provoke sexual violence by the way they dress or behave [DATA: NCRC, 2020]. This mindset is not just wrong:it is dangerous. It discourages women from reporting violence, emboldens perpetrators, and ensures that justice remains out of reach.

The Impunity Machine: Why Perpetrators Walk Free

The third lie is that the justice system works. It does not. Not for women. Not for girls. The numbers tell a grim story:

  • Only 10% of reported GBV cases in Kenya result in a conviction [DATA: Office of the Director of Public Prosecutions, 2023].
  • On average, it takes 3 years for a GBV case to be resolved in court [DATA: Judiciary of Kenya, 2022].
  • 40% of women who report GBV to the police say they were treated poorly or dismissed [DATA: Kenya National Commission on Human Rights, 2021].

Why does this happen? Because the system is broken. Police officers demand bribes to file reports. Prosecutors lose case files. Judges adjourn cases indefinitely. And all the while, perpetrators walk free, confident that the system will protect them. The Constitution says every person has the right to a fair trial [CEKA_SRC: Article 50]. But for women like Amina, the trial never even begins.

The Economic Cost of GBV: A Bill We Can’t Afford

GBV is not just a human rights issue. It is an economic one. The cost of violence against women in Kenya is estimated at 1.1% of the country’s GDP:roughly KSh 70 billion per year [DATA: World Bank, 2021]. This includes healthcare costs, lost productivity, and the long-term impact on children who grow up in violent homes. For a country struggling with debt and unemployment, this is a bill we cannot afford to keep paying.

But the real cost is not in shillings. It is in the dreams deferred. The girl who drops out of school because she is pregnant after being raped. The woman who loses her job because she is too traumatized to work. The family that slides into poverty because the breadwinner is in jail:or worse, because she is dead. The Constitution says every person has the right to the highest attainable standard of health and to social security [CEKA_SRC: Article 43]. But for women and girls experiencing GBV, these rights are a fantasy.

6. Where Is the State? The Failure of Leadership

The Silence of the State: When Leaders Look Away

The Constitution is clear: the state has a duty to protect its citizens from violence [CEKA_SRC: Article 29]. So where is the state when women are being beaten, raped, and killed? The answer is simple: missing in action.

In 2019, President Uhuru Kenyatta declared GBV a national emergency. He launched the "Generation Equality" campaign and promised to end violence against women and girls by 2026. Four years later, the emergency has only gotten worse. The number of reported GBV cases increased by 30% during the COVID-19 pandemic [DATA: Ministry of Public Service and Gender, 2021]. And yet, the state’s response has been tepid at best. Where are the safe houses for survivors? Where are the fast-track courts for GBV cases? Where is the funding for prevention programs?

The state’s failure is not just a matter of resources. It is a matter of priorities. When the government can find billions for roads and stadiums but cannot fund shelters for abused women, it sends a message: women’s lives are not a priority. The Constitution says the state shall take measures to ensure the progressive realization of economic and social rights [CEKA_SRC: Article 21]. But for women experiencing GBV, "progressive realization" is just a fancy way of saying "wait." And waiting is not an option when your life is on the line.

The Police: Part of the Problem, Not the Solution

If the state is missing in action, the police are often part of the problem. The Kenya Police Service is supposed to be the first line of defense for women experiencing violence. Instead, it is often the first hurdle. Women who report GBV to the police face a gauntlet of obstacles: officers who demand bribes, who refuse to file reports, who blame the victim, or who simply do not care.

In 2020, the Independent Policing Oversight Authority (IPOA) received 1,200 complaints of police misconduct related to GBV cases [DATA: IPOA, 2021]. This is not a few bad apples. This is a systemic failure. The Constitution says the police shall strive for the highest standards of professionalism and discipline [CEKA_SRC: Article 244]. But when it comes to GBV, the police are often the opposite of professional. They are a barrier to justice.

The Judiciary: Justice Delayed Is Justice Denied

Even when a GBV case makes it to court, the odds are stacked against the survivor. The judiciary is slow, underfunded, and overwhelmed. On average, it takes three years for a GBV case to be resolved [DATA: Judiciary of Kenya, 2022]. For a woman who has already suffered trauma, this is an eternity. Many give up. Others settle out of court, often for a fraction of what they deserve. And some simply disappear, their cases forgotten.

The Constitution says every person has the right to a fair trial without undue delay [CEKA_SRC: Article 50(2)(e)]. But for women experiencing GBV, justice delayed is justice denied. The system is not just broken:it is rigged against them.

7. What Can the Boss Do? Turning the Tide

Break the Silence: Speak Up, Speak Out

The first step in ending GBV is breaking the silence. Violence thrives in secrecy. It thrives when women are too afraid to speak, when neighbors turn a blind eye, when communities protect perpetrators. The Constitution guarantees the right to freedom of expression [CEKA_SRC: Article 33]. Use it. If you see something, say something. If you hear a woman being beaten next door, call the police. If a friend confides in you about abuse, believe her. If a man in your life thinks it’s okay to hit a woman, call him out. Silence is complicity.

Demand Accountability: Hold the State to Its Promise

The state has a duty to protect its citizens. Demand that it does. Write to your MP. Attend public forums. Hold leaders accountable for their promises. The Constitution says the state shall take measures to ensure the progressive realization of rights [CEKA_SRC: Article 21]. Demand those measures. Demand safe houses for survivors. Demand fast-track courts for GBV cases. Demand funding for prevention programs. The state works for you. Remind it of that.

Support Survivors: Be a Safe Space

Survivors of GBV need more than laws. They need support. They need someone to believe them. They need a safe place to stay. They need help navigating the justice system. If you can, volunteer at a shelter. Donate to organizations like FIDA-Kenya or the Coalition on Violence Against Women (COVAW). Offer a spare room to a woman fleeing violence. Be the person who says, "I believe you. I am here for you."

Educate the Next Generation: Teach Boys to Be Men

GBV will not end until we change the way boys are raised. Teach your sons that real men do not hit women. Teach them that consent is not optional. Teach them that women are not property. The Constitution says every person has the right to education [CEKA_SRC: Article 53]. Use that education to break the cycle of violence. Schools, churches, and communities must do better. Boys must be taught that violence is not strength. It is weakness.

Vote Like Your Life Depends on It: Because It Does

The leaders we elect determine the policies that shape our lives. Vote for leaders who prioritize women’s rights. Vote for leaders who understand that GBV is not a women’s issue:it is a national issue. The Constitution says every adult citizen has the right to vote [CEKA_SRC: Article 38]. Use that right. Vote like your life depends on it. Because for women and girls in Kenya, it does.
